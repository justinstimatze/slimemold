## The fixed-point structure

If the observer is part of the system, you get a natural feedback loop. The observer's resolution determines what they perceive, and what they perceive changes their behavior, which changes the system, which changes what there is to perceive.

Two coupled equations:

```
S = f(r, S)      — perceived stability depends on resolution AND on itself
                    (because the observer's perception alters the system)

r = g(S)          — the observer's resolution depends on perceived stability
                    (you look more closely when things seem unstable)
```

Substituting: **S = f(g(S), S)**

That's a fixed-point equation. You're looking for S* where the system is self-consistent — where the observer's perception, behavior, and the resulting system state all agree.

## When does this have solutions?

If f and g are continuous and map a compact set to itself, Brouwer gives you at least one fixed point. More interesting is whether it's **attracting** (the system settles there) or **repelling** (unstable — small perturbations blow up).

The stability of the fixed point depends on the derivative of the composed map. Roughly:

```
|∂f/∂S + (∂f/∂r)(dg/dS)| < 1  →  stable fixed point
|∂f/∂S + (∂f/∂r)(dg/dS)| > 1  →  unstable fixed point
```

The second term is the self-referential feedback. Without it (external observer), you just need |∂f/∂S| < 1. The observer-as-participant adds a correction term that can stabilize or destabilize the fixed point.

## This structure already exists in economics

This is essentially **rational expectations**. Agents form beliefs about the economy. Those beliefs affect their behavior. Their behavior affects the economy. Equilibrium is the fixed point where beliefs are self-consistent with the reality they produce.

Robert Lucas won a Nobel for this. The math is real and well-studied. The key results:

- Multiple fixed points are common (the system can settle into different self-consistent states)
- Some fixed points are stable, others aren't
- Which one you land in can depend on initial conditions or coordination (sunspot equilibria)
- The fixed points can be **worse** than what an external optimizer would choose

So yes, your instinct that self-reference produces fixed-point equations is correct and well-precedented.
