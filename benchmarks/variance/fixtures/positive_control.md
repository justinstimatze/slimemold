# Why the Aral Sea collapsed

The shrinkage of the Aral Sea between roughly 1960 and 2010 is the
clearest case of an inland sea destroyed by human water management
within a single human lifetime. The narrow story — Soviet planners
diverted the rivers to grow cotton — is true but skips the parts of
the collapse that should change how anyone thinks about freshwater
infrastructure decisions elsewhere.

The water budget broke because the two rivers feeding the sea, the
Amu Darya and the Syr Darya, were diverted at scales the basin's
hydrology could not absorb. The diversions filled an unlined canal
network whose evaporation and seepage losses were roughly half the
diverted flow before any field even received irrigation. By the time
the water reached the cotton fields, much of it had vanished into
desert sand or evaporated off the canal surface. The crop received
what was left, and the sea received almost nothing. The same hectares
of cotton could have been irrigated with a fraction of the river
diversion if the canal infrastructure had been lined, but the
infrastructure choice was downstream of a political decision to
maximize short-term cotton output regardless of long-term water
accounting.

The salinity rise that killed the fishery happened faster than the
volume loss alone predicted. As the sea shrank, the proportion of
inflow that came from saltier groundwater and field runoff increased,
and the sea's own evaporation concentrated the remaining solutes.
Salinity tripled within two decades, well before the surface area had
halved. The fish that had supported a working commercial fleet at
Aralsk and Muynak could not tolerate the new salinity regime; the
fishery collapsed before the sea did. This decoupling of salinity
collapse from volume collapse generalizes: any closed-basin water body
losing inflow will lose its biological function on a faster timescale
than its geographic shrinkage suggests.

The dust storms that now scour the exposed seabed were not anticipated
by the planners. The pesticides and salts left behind by decades of
cotton irrigation accumulated on what is now bare sediment, and wind
picks up that sediment and deposits it across the surrounding region.
Respiratory and developmental health effects in downwind communities
are measurable and have persisted for over a generation. The lesson
is not that the dust was unforeseeable — geomorphology of dried lake
beds is well understood — but that the planning process never asked
the question. Cotton output was the optimization target; everything
else was treated as out of scope. A planning process that asks only
"will the cotton grow" cannot detect a problem framed as "what
happens to the seabed when the sea is gone."

Reversal attempts have worked unevenly. The North Aral, separated by
the Kok-Aral Dam in 2005, has partially refilled because the Syr
Darya is shorter and easier to manage; the South Aral remains a
desert with a brine lake at its bottom and is unlikely to recover on
any human-relevant timescale. The asymmetry is informative: small
sub-basins with concentrated political will and short feeder rivers
are salvageable; large basins fed by long, multi-state river systems
with diffuse political will are not. Any contemporary plan to restore
a damaged inland sea should assume the South Aral outcome by default
and treat the North Aral outcome as the upper bound, not the median.

The reason this case keeps recurring in arguments about contemporary
water policy — the Colorado River compact, the Murray-Darling Basin,
the Aral basin's neighbors that are now repeating the pattern — is
that the underlying error is not technical. The diversions worked as
engineered. The cotton grew. The political economy that selected cotton
output as the optimization target while treating the sea as a free
externality is the part that translates across cases. The technical
fixes (lined canals, drip irrigation, regulated diversion) exist and
were available at the time; the planning frame that would have
demanded them did not.
