# Minutes of the Riverside Stamp Collectors' Society — March meeting

The Riverside Stamp Collectors' Society met at the Community Center on
the third Tuesday of March. Twelve members were present. The president
called the meeting to order at 7:08 PM. The minutes of the February
meeting were read by the secretary. A motion to approve the February
minutes was made by Mr. Hollis and seconded by Mrs. Pearce. The motion
carried unanimously.

The treasurer reported the current balance in the society account.
The treasurer noted that all dues for the current calendar year had
been collected. The treasurer reported one outstanding reimbursement
for refreshments from the January meeting. The reimbursement was
processed during the report. The treasurer's report was accepted by
voice vote.

The program for the evening was a presentation by Mr. Hollis on the
postal history of three coastal towns. Mr. Hollis displayed eighteen
covers from his personal collection. Mr. Hollis described the
postmarks visible on each cover. Mr. Hollis circulated three of the
covers around the table for closer inspection. The members asked
questions about the cancellation dates. The presentation concluded
at 7:54 PM.

The president opened the floor for the swap session. Mrs. Pearce
exchanged two commemoratives with Mr. Chen. Mr. Hollis and Mr. Park
traded duplicates from their respective collections. Mrs. Atwood
distributed copies of the latest auction catalog from the regional
society. Refreshments were served. The president closed the meeting
at 8:31 PM. The next meeting was confirmed for the third Tuesday of
April.

# The dining room at half past four

The light through the west window fell at a low angle across the
table. The tablecloth was white linen, recently pressed. Four places
were set. At each place was a dinner plate, a salad plate stacked on
top, a folded napkin to the left, and a water glass to the right.
The flatware was arranged with the forks on the left and the knives
and spoons on the right. A small vase of yellow flowers sat at the
center of the table. The flowers were narcissus.

The chairs were pushed in against the table. The chair at the head of
the table had a needlepoint cushion. The other three chairs had no
cushions. The floor beneath the table was hardwood. A small rug ran
the length of the table, parallel to the long edge, on the side
nearest the window. The rug was patterned in faded blue.

A clock on the sideboard ticked. The clock was wound. The time on
the clock was four-thirty-two. The sideboard was mahogany and
matched the table. Three crystal decanters stood on the sideboard,
each with a small silver tag on a chain around its neck. The tags
were inscribed in tiny lettering. The contents of the decanters were
not visible from the doorway.

A door in the wall opposite the window stood ajar. The room beyond
the door was dark. A second door, closer to the head of the table,
was closed. The closed door had a brass handle. The handle was
recently polished. No sound came from either room.

The light was beginning to lengthen across the floor. The patch of
sun that had fallen on the rug at four o'clock had moved a few inches
toward the wall. By five it would be on the wall. By half past five
it would be gone.
